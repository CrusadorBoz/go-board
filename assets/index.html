How to play

Goal — control more of the board than your opponent. You score the empty points you surround plus your stones left on the board.

  • Placing stones. Click any empty intersection. Black plays first, then turns alternate. Stones don't move once placed.
  • Liberties. A stone's liberties are the empty points directly next to it (up/down/left/right). Connected same-colour stones share their liberties as one group.
  • Capturing. Fill the last liberty of an enemy group and it is captured — removed from the board. Surround to capture.
  • No suicide. You can't place a stone that would have no liberties, unless that same move captures an enemy group.
  • Ko. You may not immediately recapture in a way that exactly repeats the previous position — play elsewhere first (the spot is marked with a small red square).
  • Passing & ending. Click Pass to skip your turn. Two passes in a row end the game and it is scored automatically.
  • Undo / redo. Use Back to step to earlier positions and Forward to replay up to the latest move. Playing a new move from a rewound spot starts a fresh line from there.
  • Scoring. Area rules: your stones + the empty regions only you surround. White gets 6.5 komi to offset Black's first move. Higher total wins.

Computer opponent — by default you play Black against a Medium computer (White). Use the controls to switch difficulty, or choose Two players to play both colours yourself, then press New game.

Tips on how to win

Winning isn't about capturing — it's about surrounding space. You win by enclosing more territory (plus your stones) than your opponent. Captures are a means, not the goal.

  • Take the corners first, then the sides. Corners need the fewest stones to wall off territory because the board edge does part of the work for you. Sides are next; the centre is the hardest place to make points. Open the game near the corners.
  • Stay a little loose in the opening. Don't pile stones right next to each other early — spread them out to stake big frameworks, then fill in. Stones two or three points apart claim far more space.
  • Keep your groups connected. Two connected groups share liberties and are hard to kill; two split groups can each be hunted down. When in doubt, link up rather than scatter.
  • Count liberties in every fight. Before a contact battle, compare liberties. If your group has more liberties than the enemy group next to it, you usually win the capturing race — if fewer, run or connect instead of adding fuel.
  • Watch for atari (one liberty left). If your group is in atari, save it (extend or capture) or sacrifice it on purpose. If the enemy is in atari, decide whether capturing actually helps or just wastes a move.
  • Make two eyes to live. A group with two separate internal empty points (two eyes) can never be captured. Groups that can't make two eyes will eventually die — so secure life for your big groups and deny it to the opponent's.
  • Don't fill your own eyes. Playing inside your own eye space turns a living group into a killable one. Leave those points empty.
  • Sente — keep the initiative. Prefer moves that force a reply (a threat to capture or cut). While the opponent is busy answering, you choose where the game goes next.
  • Use the komi. As Black you move first but White gets 6.5 points of komi. You need to net 7+ points of advantage from that first-move lead — play actively, don't just match White.
  • Endgame: take the biggest open boundary first. When the board is mostly settled, play the moves that swing the most points — usually the largest undecided borders between the two territories — before the small ones.
  • Versus the computer: it reacts move-by-move without deep planning. Build a large framework, keep your groups connected and alive, and avoid needless contact fights — steady territory beats it more reliably than trying to capture everything.

Two passes end the game. Once the borders are settled and neither side can profitably invade, pass — if your enclosed area plus stones (and komi) is higher, you win.
